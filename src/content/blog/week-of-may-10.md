## A war is brewing over lithium mining near Death Valley

[![](https://californiacurated.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/05/lithuum.png)](<https://www.latimes.com/local/california/la-me-death-valley-lithium-mine-california-environment-20190507-story.html>)

Lithium. It is one of the world's most valuable elements, allowing batteries to be more powerful and longer-lasting than ever before. Right now, most lithium is mined in the high deserts of South America, but a new battle is being waged between battery companies and environmentalists over whether to mine lithium in Panamint Valley in California, right on the edge of [Death Valley](<https://www.latimes.com/local/california/la-me-death-valley-lithium-mine-california-environment-20190507-story.html>). There are strong arguments to be made that having a large domestic source of lithium is key to a carbon-free future, but some are saying that mining would potentially despoil one of California's most treasured natural areas. 

The LA Times [has a story](<https://www.latimes.com/local/california/la-me-death-valley-lithium-mine-california-environment-20190507-story.html>) on how Australia-based firm Battery Mineral Resources Ltd. is seeking [permission to drill four exploratory wells](<https://eplanning.blm.gov/epl-front-office/projects/nepa/120541/168863/205513/Panamint_EA_20190308_508.pdf>) beneath the valley floor to see if enough lithium is there to make a mine economically viable.

## The comeback of Mark Twain's frogs

[![](https://californiacurated.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/05/frogs.jpg)](<https://www.kqed.org/science/1941283/mark-twains-celebrated-red-legged-frogs-thriving-in-yosemite-valley>)Red-Legged Frog Release.

The California red-legged frog is said to be the species featured in Twain's short story "The Celebrated Jumping Frog of Calaveras County."

They began to disappear decades ago due to disease and habitat destruction, but a recent [program to reintroduce them back in Yosemite](<https://www.kqed.org/science/1941283/mark-twains-celebrated-red-legged-frogs-thriving-in-yosemite-valley>) Valley is seeing some progress. The program reintroduced about 4,000 California red-legged frog eggs and tadpoles and 500 adult frogs, into Yosemite and near the Merced River. For the first time, biologists have found eggs from the reintroduced frogs. That's great news, given the rapidly declining state of frogs around the globe. The recent [IPBES UN report](<https://www.ipbes.net/global-assessment-experts>) says that more than 40 percent of amphibian species around the world are threatened with extinction.

## Science of traffic jams 

[![](https://californiacurated.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/05/traffic.png)](<http://nautil.us//issue/71/flow/why-a-traffic-flow-suddenly-turns-into-a-traffic-jam>)Credit: Erik Olsen

Traffic jams. They are the bane of California drivers. But what causes them, and is there any way to lessen their severity? Mathematicians have developed all sorts of models to better understand how traffic forms, and some of them has been helpful to improve flow. For example, extra-long freeway entry lanes (take a drive on Highway 110, the old Route 66, which has very short entry lanes, to see what I'm talking about.) An [interesting story](<http://nautil.us//issue/71/flow/why-a-traffic-flow-suddenly-turns-into-a-traffic-jam>) in [Nautilus](<http://nautil.us>) examines how fluid models are being used to better predict and reduce traffic jams. It's complicated, but you will learn about the[ jamiton](<https://math.mit.edu/projects/traffic/>). And we're not holding our breath that things will improve in places like LA anytime soon.

## Gulls of Mono Lake

[![](https://californiacurated.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/05/science-friday-gif-downsized_large-15.gif)](<https://www.sciencefriday.com/videos/to-see-gulls-anew/>)

Kristie Nelson studies seagulls at Mono Lake, home to massive colonies of gulls. Her Mono Lake Gull Project examines how gulls serve as an indicator of ecosystem health. The gulls spend most of their time at the coast, but during breeding season they make fly to saline places like Mono Lake where the population can reach up to 65,000 birds.   
  
A [video at Science Friday](<https://www.sciencefriday.com/videos/to-see-gulls-anew/>) looks at her work and has some great scenes of the voracious birds going after the lake's insanely numerous Alkali Flies, moving across the bazillions of them, beaks open, like a lawnmower.

## Scallop eyes surprise scientists

[![](https://californiacurated.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/05/scallops.jpg)](<https://www.smithsonianmag.com/science-nature/what-scallops-many-eyes-can-teach-us-about-evolution-vision-180972099/>)Wikipedia

Many people know that scallops have eyes, blue ones, in fact. But their eyes function a bit differently than our own. As light enters into the scallop eye, it goes through the pupil and then a lens. Interestingly, the scallop has two retinas, and when the light hits them it strikes a crystal mirror made of guanine at the back of the eye. 

A [study in Current Biology](<https://www.cell.com/current-biology/fulltext/S0960-9822\(19\)30381-1>) looks at two species: the bay scallop _Argopecten irradian_ s and the sea scallop _Placopecten magellanicus,_ and reveals that scallops have a novel way of focusing light. They have no irises like ours and so they use their pupils to dilate and contract, and this, along with changes to the curvature of the cornea, improves resolution and forms crisper images. Vision is such an amazingly complex ability, yet it has likely evolved 50 times among animals, a process called convergent evolution. There are several scallop species in California, and the next time you are diving and see one, remember that it probably sees you right back.

## Reducing cow burps with seaweed

![](https://californiacurated.com/wp-content/uploads/2019/05/cow-field-one-health-uc-davis.jpg)UC Davis

You've seen Harris Ranch on I5? Did you know that California is a major producer of beef and dairy. Cows produce prodigious amounts of methane, one of the most potent greenhouse gases. In fact, methane is 30 times worse than CO2. Meanwhile, [more than half](<https://www.arb.ca.gov/cc/inventory/background/ch4.htm>) of all methane emissions in California come from the burps, farts, and exhalations of livestock. And belches are the worst, accounting for roughly 95% of the methane released into the environment. Worldwide, livestock accounts for 16% of our greenhouse gas emissions. A fascinating new approach at [Scripps Institution of Oceanography](<https://twitter.com/Scripps_Ocean>) proposes using seaweed as cow feed. Scripps notes that "just a small amount of  _Asparagopsis_ seaweed to cattle feed can dramatically reduce methane emissions from dairy cows by more than 50 percent".
